*A Parent's Guide to Research-Supported Nutritional Interventions*

## Executive Summary

Several nutritional supplements have demonstrated meaningful benefits for children with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD) and/or Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder (ADHD) in randomized controlled trials. While none replace conventional treatment, omega-3 fatty acids, targeted minerals (zinc, iron), and specific vitamins can serve as valuable adjunctsâ€”particularly when underlying deficiencies are identified. This document summarizes the current evidence to help families make informed decisions alongside their healthcare providers.

## Before You Start: What to Test

Before beginning any supplement regimen, consider testing for deficiencies that are common in ASD/ADHD populations and may guide treatment:

| Test | Why It Matters | Notes |
|------|----------------|-------|
| **Serum Ferritin (Iron)** | Children with ADHD frequently have lower iron stores | Supplement *only* if deficientâ€”excess iron is harmful |
| **Serum Zinc** | Zinc deficiency linked to ADHD severity | Low levels predict better response to zinc supplementation |
| **25-OH Vitamin D** | Often low in ASD; affects behavior | Dosing should be based on blood levels |
| **Folate Receptor Autoantibodies (FRAAs)** | Present in subset of ASD children | Predicts response to folinic acid therapy |

## Safety Notes & Interactions

### General Principles
- **More is not better.** Stick to studied doses.
- **Test before supplementing** iron and zincâ€”toxicity is possible.
- **Allow adequate time.** Most supplements need 8â€“14+ weeks to show effects.
- **Inform all providers.** Keep your child's doctor aware of all supplements.

### Specific Cautions

| Supplement | Safety Consideration |
|------------|---------------------|
| **Iron** | Never supplement without confirmed deficiency; can be toxic |
| **Zinc** | High doses can deplete copper; use balanced formulations |
| **NAC** | May interact with nitroglycerin; generally well-tolerated |
| **Saffron** | Limited long-term safety data; avoid in pregnancy |
| **Omega-3s** | High doses may increase bleeding risk; pause before surgery |
| **Folinic Acid** | Different from folic acidâ€”ensure correct form |

### Drug Interactions
- **Zinc + Stimulants:** May allow lower stimulant dosing (coordinate with prescriber)
- **Omega-3s + Blood Thinners:** Monitor for increased bleeding
- **NAC + Activated Charcoal:** NAC may be inactivated

1. **Omega-3s are first-line** for both conditionsâ€”well-studied, safe, and affordable.

2. **Test for deficiencies** in iron, zinc, vitamin D, and folate receptor antibodies before supplementing.

3. **Zinc as a stimulant adjunct** can reduce medication needs by over a third in deficient children.

4. **Probiotics help behavior but not core ASD symptoms**â€”manage expectations accordingly.

5. **Saffron shows promise for ADHD** but needs more research before routine recommendation.

6. **Always involve your healthcare provider**â€”supplements can interact with medications and should be part of a comprehensive treatment plan.
